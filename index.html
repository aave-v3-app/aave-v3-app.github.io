What is Aave?

Aave is a decentralised non-custodial liquidity protocol where suppliers provide liquidity and borrowers access liquidity by providing collateral, as described in the Aave documentation. The official interface also exposes swap tools for tokens and position management where supported.

Aave V3 supply APY and borrow rates

Aave V3 supply APY and borrow rates are market-based, not fixed. Suppliers earn interest from borrowers, and borrow rates rise or fall with utilization of each reserve. If a pool has high utilization, borrowing becomes more expensive and supplier yield can rise; if liquidity is abundant, both rates can fall. Aave docs describe a utilization-based interest rate model with an optimal utilization point and steeper rates above that point. For searchers, the only accurate APY is the live rate for a specific asset, market, and chain in the Aave app. Stablecoin supply may be a few percent in normal conditions, but it can move quickly.

Aave V3 health factor and liquidation

Aave V3 health factor measures whether a borrowing position has enough collateral relative to its debt. If the health factor falls below 1, the position becomes eligible for liquidation. That can happen when collateral prices fall, borrowed assets rise, interest accrues, or collateral parameters change. A liquidator can repay part of the debt and receive collateral with a liquidation bonus defined for the reserve. Borrowers should track health factor, liquidation threshold, loan-to-value, and oracle prices rather than only watching the dollar value of collateral. The practical goal is to keep a margin above 1 that fits the volatility of the assets used.

Aave V3 collateral assets and E-Mode

Aave V3 supports collateral and borrowing by market, reserve, and chain. Not every listed asset can be used the same way: each reserve has parameters such as loan-to-value, liquidation threshold, supply cap, borrow cap, isolation settings, and reserve factor. E-Mode is designed for correlated assets, such as certain stablecoins or ETH-correlated assets, and can improve capital efficiency when the supplied and borrowed assets are in the same category. Isolation Mode limits riskier collateral by restricting what can be borrowed and applying debt ceilings. Users should check live reserve parameters before using any asset as collateral.

Aave V3 flash loans

Aave V3 flash loans let developers borrow liquidity and repay it within the same transaction. If the transaction does not repay according to the protocol rules, the whole transaction reverts. This design is mainly for developers building arbitrage, collateral swaps, liquidations, migrations, or other atomic DeFi operations. It is not a normal consumer loan and does not remove the need for smart contract execution knowledge. Aave's documentation includes flash loan guides and contract interfaces, but users should treat flash loans as an advanced feature because errors can waste gas or expose contracts to execution risk.

Aave GHO and sGHO

GHO is the Aave ecosystem stablecoin, while sGHO refers to Savings GHO, a yield-oriented form described in Aave's documentation. GHO is separate from AAVE, the governance token, and separate from aTokens, which represent supplied assets in Aave markets. Aave users may encounter GHO as a borrowable or usable asset depending on the market and governance configuration. Searchers should check the live app for GHO liquidity, borrow terms, and savings rate because stablecoin usage is parameter-driven and can change through governance and market conditions.

Aave FAQ

What happens when you supply assets to Aave V3?

When you supply an asset to Aave V3, the protocol mints an interest-bearing aToken for that asset, such as aUSDC or aWETH. The aToken represents your supplied position and accrues value as borrowers pay interest into the reserve. You can usually withdraw the underlying asset when there is enough unborrowed liquidity and your withdrawal does not make any active borrow position unsafe.

Can you lose collateral on Aave?

Yes. A borrower can lose collateral through liquidation if the position's health factor falls below 1. Liquidation happens when collateral is no longer sufficient for the debt under Aave's reserve parameters and oracle prices. The liquidator repays part of the debt and receives collateral with a bonus. This is why borrowers keep a buffer and monitor health factor, not just loan size.

What is E-Mode in Aave V3?

E-Mode, or Efficiency Mode, is an Aave V3 feature for correlated assets. When a user supplies and borrows assets inside the same E-Mode category, the protocol can allow higher capital efficiency than normal parameters. A common example is stablecoin-to-stablecoin borrowing. The feature can improve borrowing power, but it also concentrates risk around assets that are assumed to remain correlated.

What is Isolation Mode in Aave V3?

Isolation Mode lets Aave list newer or riskier collateral assets while limiting system-wide exposure. If a user supplies an isolated collateral asset, borrowing is restricted to specific assets, usually stablecoins, and a debt ceiling applies. The goal is to allow more collateral listings without letting one volatile or less-proven asset create unlimited risk across the whole market.

Does Aave V3 have fixed APY?

No. Aave V3 APY changes by asset, chain, utilization, reserve parameters, and incentives. Supplier yield comes from borrower interest net of protocol reserve factors, while borrow rates follow the market's interest rate strategy. A rate visible today can be different later. Users should compare the live supply APY, borrow APR, incentives, liquidity, and health-factor impact before entering a position.

What is the AAVE token for?

AAVE is the governance token for the Aave ecosystem. It is distinct from aTokens, which represent supplied assets, and from GHO, the Aave ecosystem stablecoin. AAVE holders can participate in governance processes that influence protocol upgrades, markets, parameters, and ecosystem decisions. Buying AAVE does not automatically supply funds to Aave V3 or earn lending interest.
